# NPS Is Overused. Build a Better CX Metric Stack.

Net Promoter Score is widely treated as the customer experience metric, but one survey question and one headline number are too simple to carry that much weight. The appeal is obvious. The decision quality is not.

## Why NPS Became the Default

NPS is attractive because it compresses sentiment into a single number that executives can track on a dashboard.

## How NPS Works

Respondents answer a 0 to 10 recommendation question. Scores of 9 to 10 are promoters, 7 to 8 are passives, and 0 to 6 are detractors. NPS is the percentage of promoters minus the percentage of detractors.

## What NPS Throws Away

That calculation discards the middle of the distribution and hides whether the customer base is calm, polarized, or volatile.

## The Distribution Problem

Two companies can report the same NPS with very different score patterns. One can have a twin-peaks shape with many fans and many critics. Another can cluster around 7s and 8s. The headline number hides that difference.

## Statistical Problems

NPS is the difference between two noisy proportions. It can swing hard on small samples, and those swings often get treated as meaningful changes when they are mostly noise.

## Intent Is Not Behavior

The survey asks whether someone would recommend the brand, not whether they actually did. Intent and action often diverge, and the same customer can recommend and warn against a company at different times.

## How Organizations Misuse It

When targets, bonuses, or team status get tied to NPS, people start gaming the survey, coaching respondents, and chasing the number instead of fixing customer problems.

## How to Use NPS More Carefully

If a team keeps NPS, it should always inspect the full score distribution, track mean and median, watch extreme buckets, and read the qualitative why-comments instead of treating the delta as sufficient.

## Better Metrics to Pair With NPS

A more useful customer experience stack combines NPS with CSAT, CES, behavioral data, journey analytics, retention, churn, repeat purchase, expansion, and task-level outcomes.

## Practical Recommendation

Do not ban NPS. Use it as a rough sentiment signal, not a KPI deity. The practical move is to use a small set of complementary metrics to prioritize experiments and fixes instead of declaring victory from one number.
