Net Promoter Score is widely treated as the customer experience metric, but one survey question and one headline number are too simple to carry that much weight. The appeal is obvious. The decision quality is not.

## Core Question
Why is NPS too weak to serve as the main customer experience KPI on its own, and what should a more useful measurement stack look like for technical business teams?

## Audience
Technical business users, operators, product leads, and analytically minded managers who need to understand customer experience without reducing it to a misleading dashboard number.

## Reader Outcome
By the end of the piece, the reader should understand:
- why NPS is attractive and why that simplicity creates blind spots
- how distribution shape, sample size, and intent-versus-behavior gaps weaken NPS as a standalone KPI
- how teams misuse NPS when they attach status, incentives, or strategy to a single number
- what a practical CX metric stack looks like instead

Keep the prose conversational and business-aware, but technically credible. Favor plain English, concrete examples, operational implications, and crisp judgments.
